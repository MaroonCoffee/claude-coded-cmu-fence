The CMU Fence

Carnegie Mellon's most iconic canvas — a student-centered space for free expression since 1923.

📜 The Official Rules
🎓

CMU Students Only

The Fence may only be painted by Carnegie Mellon University-affiliated students. Only students guarding the Fence, and those they willingly associate with, may paint.

🕛

Midnight to Sunrise

The Fence must be painted between midnight and sunrise only. Any painting outside this timeframe is considered vandalism, excluding corrective work to undo vandalism.

👥

Guarded at All Times

The Fence is considered guarded when at least two students are in the surrounding gravel area. Guards must remain at all times during painting.

🖌️

Brushes Only

The Fence may only be hand-painted with paint brushes. No spray paint, rollers, or other methods are permitted.

🎨

Fully Covered

The Fence must be completely covered with paint. Partial paintings are considered vandalism.

🧹

Clean Up After Yourself

All paint and painting materials must stay in the gravel area surrounding the Fence. Painters are liable for cleanup of all resources used.

📖 History of The Fence

The Fence is an iconic piece of Carnegie Mellon's Pittsburgh campus. Serving as a student-centered space for free expression and community, The Fence has brought the CMU community together for more than 100 years.

The earliest documented reference to the idea of "a fence" was in 1913. However, the building of The Fence was pushed to 1923, when the class of 1923 built what they referred to as the Senior Fence — originally built for seniors, by seniors.

By the 1960s, the tradition of painting the fence was in full swing. In 1989, the west end collapsed and was replaced; in 1993, the east end followed. Today, the Fence is for all students at Carnegie Mellon.

1923
Class of 1923 builds the "Senior Fence" — originally reserved for seniors only.
1960s
The tradition of painting the fence becomes established campus-wide.
1974
The Fence is painted when President Nixon resigned.
1980
The Fence was painted with "Imagine" lyrics as a memorial to John Lennon.
1989
The west end of the Fence collapsed and was replaced with pressure-treated lumber and concrete.
1993
The east end of The Fence followed suit with reconstruction.
2001
On 9/11, The Fence was painted in remembrance of victims. It doubled as a gathering spot for a candlelight vigil.
2008
After the passing of CMU Professor Randy Pausch, The Fence was painted in his honor.
2011
"Operation Heal the Fence" attracted hundreds after first-year art students damaged The Fence with a hacksaw.
2020
During COVID-19, The Fence was painted with "CYA on Zoom." The "Black Lives Matter" message was quickly restored after being changed.
2024
Student Government helped cement core tenets of The Fence — free expression, respect, and student-centered foundation.
