📖 About The Fence

100+ years of CMU tradition — rules, history, and community guidelines.

🎨 What is The Fence?

The Fence is an iconic piece of Carnegie Mellon's Pittsburgh campus, serving as a student-centered space for free expression and community for more than 100 years.

Governed by the Student Government Graffiti and Poster Policy, The Fence has specific expectations regarding how it may be painted. Adherence to the policy helps keep the tradition alive for future Tartans.

Questions about The Fence? Email the Student Government Poster Policy Arbiter.

📋 Painting Rules & Guidelines

👤 Who Can Paint The Fence?

  • → The Fence may only be painted by Carnegie Mellon University-affiliated students. [Article II.D.6]
  • → Only students guarding The Fence, and those with whom they are willingly associating, may paint. [Article II.D.6]
  • → The Fence is considered guarded when at least two students are in the surrounding gravel area. [Article II.D.6.a]

🖌️ How Can You Paint?

  • → The Fence may only be hand-painted with paint brushes. [Article II.D.1.a]
  • → No spray paint, rollers, dumping, or smearing paint are permitted. [Article II.D.1.b]
  • → The Fence must be completely covered with paint — partial paintings are considered vandalism. [Article II.D.1.c]

🌙 When Can You Paint?

  • → The Fence must be painted between midnight and sunrise. [Article II.D.5]
  • → Any painting outside that timeframe is considered vandalism, except corrective work to undo vandalism. [Article II.D.5]

🧹 Keeping The Fence Area Clean

  • → All paint and painting materials must stay in the gravel area surrounding The Fence. Any paint outside this area is considered graffiti. [Article II.D.2-3]
  • → Those painting The Fence are liable for cleanup of all resources used. [Article II.D.4]
⚠️ Concerns at The Fence

Respect for fellow students and The Fence itself is key to ensuring this time-honored tradition continues. Student Government is committed to making sure all students and organizations can freely participate.

The Poster Policy Arbiter (PPA) is a student employed by Student Government to intake concerns, help identify solutions, and encourage open and restorative dialogue.

📜 History of The Fence

How It Started

The history of The Fence is often shrouded in misinformation. After university archives and student research, the earliest documented reference to the idea of "a fence" was in 1913. However, with major world events and ebbing interest, the building of The Fence was pushed to 1923, when the class of 1923 built what they referred to as the Senior Fence. Originally, The Fence was built for seniors, by seniors.

Reinforcing The Fence

During the Senior Fence era, the main tradition was for seniors to sit at the fence and celebrate — non-seniors were reprimanded. That tradition slowly faded, giving way to the tradition we know today: painting The Fence. By the 1960s the tradition was in full swing.

After many decades (and layers of paint), in 1989, the west end collapsed and was replaced with pressure-treated lumber and concrete. In 1993, the east end followed. While the original Senior Fence may no longer exist, today The Fence belongs to all students at Carnegie Mellon University.

Key Moments in History

1923
Class of 1923 builds the original Senior Fence as a gathering place for graduating seniors.
1960s
Painting the Fence becomes the dominant tradition, open to the whole campus community.
1974
The Fence is painted to mark President Nixon's resignation — one of the earliest records of Fence as political expression.
1980
"Imagine" lyrics painted as a memorial to John Lennon following his passing.
1989 & 1993
Both ends of the original Fence are rebuilt with pressure-treated lumber and concrete, preserving the tradition.
2001
On September 11, the Fence is painted in remembrance and doubles as a candlelight vigil gathering point for the CMU community.
2008
The Fence is painted in honor of Professor Randy Pausch, author of "The Last Lecture," following his passing.
2011
"Operation Heal the Fence" draws hundreds of students after vandalism with a hacksaw — the community rallies to restore it.
2020
"CYA on Zoom" — a COVID-19 era painting captures the universal experience of the pandemic semester.
2020
When a "Black Lives Matter" message is altered, students mobilize overnight to restore it — demonstrating community values.
2024
Permanent plaques are installed near The Fence by Student Government and university leadership, codifying its core values of free expression and community.
📱 The Fence Instagram

Though not an official Student Government account, @cmufence on Instagram has served as an important way for the CMU community to engage with The Fence. By encouraging painters to submit photos and information via Google Form, this account provides first-person context for designs.

The Instagram also serves as an unofficial historical record for recent Fence designs — a unique way for CMU community members outside Pittsburgh, including alumni, to experience The Fence.
